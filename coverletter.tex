\begin{cvletter}

I studied Computer Science during my undergraduate degree, and it fundamentally changed how I see the world.

Before then,  computers were little more than sophisticated entertainment devices to me---tools for playing music, watching films, or browsing the internet. I had no real sense of what lay beneath the surface. That changed quickly. During my studies, I learned not only how computers work, but how to program them and, more importantly, how to harness their power.

I was struck by their capability. Complex calculations could be completed instantly; retrieving the 500th digit of pi was trivial. Beyond raw computational speed, what fascinated me was their consistency---machines that could execute millions of tasks with the same precision from the first to the last, operate continuously on schedule, and store vast amounts of knowledge. It felt like magic. The potential seemed limitless.

And yet, despite decades of progress, the world remains far from perfect.

Over time, I came to understand that technology is not defined by how impressive it is in isolation, but by the impact it has on people's lives. The steam engine did not transform society merely because it was an engineering marvel; it did so because it reshaped productivity and brought distant places closer together. Its true value lay in the tangible improvements it created.

This realization shaped my career path. After graduating, I moved into product management, where I could focus not just on building technology, but on applying it meaningfully.

In this role, I learned to bridge perspectives. Conversations with leadership helped me understand long-term vision and strategic direction, while discussions with employees on the ground revealed the daily inefficiencies and frustrations that often go unseen. It was in connecting these viewpoints that real opportunities emerged. Only after clearly defining the problem and envisioning a solution did technology enter the equation---as an enabler, not the starting point.

When done well, the results were powerful: increased efficiency, improved job satisfaction, and better allocation of time. I built my career around this process---understanding problems, designing solutions, and bringing together teams to deliver them.

For a time, this felt complete.

Then I began to notice the early signs of a new technological shift.

At first, it appeared as a curiosity---a simple interface where one could ask questions and receive answers. But it quickly became clear that this was something fundamentally different. The responses were not only accurate, but contextual, adaptive, and increasingly human-like. Soon came code generation, image creation, and multimodal systems---each advancing at an extraordinary pace. It became evident that we were entering the age of artificial intelligence.

I found myself experiencing the same sense of wonder I had felt when I first encountered computers. Once again, I was faced with a technology so powerful that it seemed almost magical.

But I have learned this lesson before: the true value of a technology lies not in its sophistication, but in its application.

Today, we are only beginning to understand how to translate advances in AI, data, and digital technologies into meaningful, large-scale impact. Many organizations are investing in these capabilities, yet struggle to integrate them effectively into decision-making, processes, and business models. The challenge is no longer access to technology, but the ability to transform it into value.

This is precisely where I see my future.

I want to operate at the intersection of data, technology, and business transformation---identifying opportunities where intelligent systems can solve real-world problems, and ensuring that their implementation creates measurable impact. To do this effectively, I need to deepen both my technical understanding of data and AI systems, and my ability to design, govern, and implement them within complex organizations.

The Master's in Information Management with a specialization in Digital Transformation at NOVA IMS represents the ideal environment for this next step.

The program's focus on data-driven decision-making, information systems, governance, and emerging technologies aligns directly with the direction I want to grow into. Its emphasis on transforming business processes and models through the strategic use of data and AI reflects the exact challenge I aim to tackle in my career. I am particularly drawn to the integration of technical and managerial perspectives, as well as the program's strong connection to real-world applications.

Equally important is the opportunity to learn alongside peers who bring diverse perspectives and ambitions, in an environment that encourages collaboration and practical problem-solving. I believe this ecosystem is essential for developing the kind of intuition required to navigate rapidly evolving technologies.

I do not want to observe this transformation from the sidelines. I want to be part of it---to contribute, to build, and to help ensure that this new form of ``magic'' is translated into meaningful, lasting impact.

The NOVA IMS program offers me the foundation, perspective, and community to do exactly that.

\end{cvletter}
